Judging the Germ Theory: Public Health in the Progressive Era

Seminar Description
The advent of the germ theory in the late-nineteenth century had important consequences for the ways cities attempted to protect the public's health. For instance in 1907 the New York City health department determined that a thirty-seven-year-old Irish cook named Mary Mallon had infected more than twenty people with typhoid fever and was a public health menace. She disagreed but was nevertheless forced to spend the rest of her life incarcerated at Riverside Hospital on North Brother Island, New York. Why was this curtailment of individual liberty deemed acceptable? In this seminar, we will explore how health officials, physicians, Progressive Era reformers, and the public understood the germ theory and the contests that ensued because of disagreements in interpretation. We will consider how perceptions of gender, ethnicity, class, and race influenced public health policy. We will also examine how cases like Typhoid Mary's fit into the history of social regulation of work, housing, and public safety in the general context of Progressive Era reforms.

In January, she will become Assistant Professor of History and Director of the Center for Public History at Florida State University. She is currently working on a book entitled Eden's Underbelly: Women, Public Health Reform, and State-Making in Early Twentieth-Century Los Angeles.

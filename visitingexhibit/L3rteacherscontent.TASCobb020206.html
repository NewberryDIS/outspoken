Crusade and Jihad

Seminar Description
Between 1095 and 1291, waves of European Christians participated in various episodes of conquest and settlement in the heart if the Islamic world. Muslims, for their part, initiated an equally robust response by calling for jihad, or holy war, against the invaders. Where do these seemingly contradictory ideals (violence, piety, pilgrimage) come from, and how did they combine into concepts of crusade and jihad? What were the nonviolent aspects of Muslim-Christian confrontation during the Crusades, and what were the roles of the "minor players" in the drama, such as the Jewish and eastern Christian communities? By reading and discussing primary sources together, participants will appreciate the very different impacts that the Crusades had upon Europeans and the native populations of the Middle East and will be in a better position to evaluate the significance of the Crusades, far removed from the usual extreme characterizations of "heroic" or "demonic" that these movements have tended to attract.

About the Instructor
Paul Cobb is Associate Professor of History and Fellow of the Medieval Institute at the University of Notre Dame. He is a specialist in medieval Islam and Islamic perspectives on the Crusades. Cobb is currently translating the memoirs of two medieval Muslim eyewitnesses to the Crusades: the warrior Usama Ibn Munqidh and the noted traveller Ibn Jubayr.

National History Project

Investigating Pullman's Approach to Labor Issues

Researched by Richard Mertz, Oak Park-River Forest School

        When I received the letter indicating that I had been accepted to attend the Newberry’s National History Project, I was excited about the opportunity to do primary source research as well as discuss historical topics with other teachers.   The NHP allowed me the freedom to pick any America history topic and I initially wanted to gain some insight into the Red Scare that took place during and after the first World War.  I had always questioned the sincerity of the Red Scare during the early 1920s.  The 1950s Scare seemed reasonable given the very real new threat of atomic warfare; but not the World War I Scare.  Therefore, I wanted to research the role of management in exploiting the Red Scare to attack organized labor.

    My research took me in several directions. I examined at some length newspaper articles and magazines from the 1910s and 1920s.  After spending two straight days, eight hours each day, scanning microfilm at Harold Washington Library, I came to the conclusion that the job of a historian is tedious.  What I discovered was that every time there was a strike, editorials blasted the labor movement for being connected with communism/bolshevism.  I got frustrated.  Everywhere I looked, I failed to find any interesting information.  I realized that the answer to my question was “yes,” management did use the Red Scare to attack all unions whether or not they were radical. There was no subtlety; I could offer no real insight into the topic. 

    I was just about to give up when I came across a reference in the Pullman collection to employee representation plans. At first I was a bit confused.  Why was Pullman, a company that was notoriously anti-union, allowing employee representation plans?  After reading several secondary sources, I discovered that during the 1920s, management sought to control unionism by offering workers an alternative to collective bargaining.  With this new idea in hand, I set out to see to what extent this was true in the Pullman Company.  The evidence I found was overwhelming.  Pullman implemented a wide range of programs for the purpose of developing employee loyalty to the company.   I discovered that the topic was not without subtlety.  Even though management sought to attack labor, workers did benefit under the new approach.  I came to the conclusion that during and immediately after World War I, labor and management took the theme of democracy and applied it to the workplace.  The war had transformed the worker and society, making the traditional anti-labor approach ineffective. 

    For instance, the Pullman Company established a company newsletter in May of 1922 entitled Pullman News.  Articles included birth and death announcements, baseball, basketball and bowling scores, recipes, and entertainment pieces.  The newsletter also included information of employee benefits, election results and worker commendations as well as personal ads from the various precincts. 

    However, the paper also served management’s interests. After examining many issues, it became clear that the Pullman News was a management propaganda tool that advocated the themes of cooperation and loyalty.  For example, an article entitled “Ex-office boy Now Assistant General Manager,” told the classic Horatio Alger story of how hard work and cooperation will lead to success.(1) (See Document One) Similarly, an address from President Carry, not only expressed his own rags to riches story but emphasized cooperation and loyalty.(2) (See Document Two)

    Almost as interesting as what was printed, is what was absent from the Pullman News – the topic of organized labor. In the midst of the 1922 Shopman’s strike, the Pullman press made no reference to it.  In fact, in the eight years of newsletters I examined, there were no articles on the evil of unionism even though the subtext of cooperation and loyalty to the company was anti-union. 

    In addition to the Pullman News, the Company offered a wide range of social activities to foster a community spirit, including baseball, basketball and bowling leagues as well as a choral group.   It also sponsored contests and company picnics.

    Pullman also offered a range of economic reforms.  In 1920, Pullman instituted a Savings Plan for all of its workers.  The goal was to offer a better return for the worker since their collective savings would bring a higher return than an individual savings plan would.  The plan had the additional goal of creating loyal, stable workers.  In a letter dated October 25, 1922, President Cary stated “I have always felt that a workman with a bank balance is more conservative in his view and inclined to work more steadily … than the man with nothing at stake.” (3) (See Document Three) Pullman also instituted a stock ownership plan.   

    Probably the greatest tool at controlling the labor question was the adoption of the Employee Representation Plan.  The plan structure gave employees a voice in the management of the company.   These plans created a three-tier system of representation (the Local Committee, the General Committee and the Bureau of Industrial Relations) with equal representation of employees and management.  The local committees heard employee grievances, and if the employee was not satisfied with the decision, she or he could appeal it to the next level. The stated purpose of this plan was “to promote co-operation between the management and the employees and to provide for handling expeditiously and settling promptly and fairly all questions which arise as to current wages and working conditions and other matters of importance to the welfare of the employees.”   While the stated purpose seems benevolent, Frank Simmons, the director of labor relations, gives an alternative purpose for a strong Employee Representations Plan:

… My own individual belief is that during this period of labor unrest and of extreme activity on the part of various socialistic and communistic organizations, it behooves the Company to strengthen and popularize its own Plan of Employee Representation as the channel through which employees will have representation and a voice in matters affecting their welfare, and if we do not do this there is great danger, proved by our own experience, that our employees will drift into other and antagonistic organizations which are constantly soliciting their membership. (4) (See Document Four) By 1922, Pullman had instituted an Employee Representation Plan for all of its workers with the hope of dissuading them from joining organized labor.

    Did ERP's grievance procedures offer workers a place to settle their complaints? In examining the data for the years 1926 to 1934, I discovered some interesting trends. (5)(See Document Five)  Complaints that reached the local committee level were more likely to be decided in favor of the employee (43% to 36.9%).  However, at higher levels management was more often successful (43% to 39%).  More importantly, management received favorable decisions more often than did employees in discharge complaints. In discharge cases, the numbers are 463 to 249 in favor of management.  Thus, the Employee Representative Plan allowed workers a marginal level of control and input.
 
    Under the guise of industrial democracy, Pullman instituted the above reforms aimed at marginally increasing the role of the worker without losing control of the workplace to organized labor’s view of industrial democracy.

    A final note about the experience. The summer institute helped me gain insights into the study of history and provided me with the opportunity to explore the nuances of labor history. I also appreciated the intellectual camaraderie of the other participants, reminding me of my days in college. As a teacher, I wanted to stretch my intellectual curiosity and reconnect to academics and I did.

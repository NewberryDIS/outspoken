Exchange Before Orientalism

Seminar Description
It is natural that, as Americans, we think first of European-New World encounters when we think of the age of exploration. Yet this period also saw an unprecedented level of contact between Europe and Asia, contact that both reinforced and challenged European countries' identities. In this seminar we will investigate the degree and variety of exchange that existed between Europe and Asia between 1500 and 1800. This time frame allows us to examine exchange before European economic and technological advantages set the scene for turning such encounters into expressions of unequal power relations. We will discuss common secondary readings and examine many primary sources, such as maps, texts, and images-many of them from the Newberry's collections-to better understand these early modern European and Asian encounters.

About the Instructors
Laura Hostetler is Associate Professor in the Department of History at the University of Illinois at Chicago. She specializes in the history of China during the early modern period. Her first book, Qing Colonial Enterprise, deals with the use of cartography and ethnography in the process of empire building in China. Her current research explores Chinese contacts with Europe, focusing primarily on France during the late seventeenth and early eighteenth centuries.

Ellen McClure is Associate Professor in the Department of French, Spanish, Italian and Portuguese at the University of Illinois at Chicago. She is the author of Sunspots and the Sun King: Sovereignty and Mediation in Seventeenth-Century France. She has written extensively on early modern diplomacy, both within Europe and between Europe and other regions. Her expertise includes French contacts and interactions with the Ottoman empire and Siam and their representation in literature, particularly during the reign of Louis XIV.

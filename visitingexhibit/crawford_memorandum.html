Memorandum for D. A. Crawford by F. L. Simmons, dated November 18, 1922

    Referring to the President’s Memorandum of October 25th, 1922 addressed to Mr. Lane, relative to Savings Club.

    While in the East recently I made inquiries to ascertain what other industries are doing in the way of Savings Clubs.

    I found that in 1890 certain employees of the New York Central Railroad and of the American Express Company organized the “Railroad Co-operative Building and Loan Association”. This association has not been in successful operation under the supervision of the Banking Department of the state of New York for over 32 years and has resources amounting to $5,300,000 and a surplus and undivided profits of $130,000.

    It is entirely separate and distinct from the New York Central Railroad; its only connection with the Railroad Company is that the majority of its officers and directors are officials or employees of the New York Central Railroad, although the directorate includes some officials of the American Express Company and of the American Locomotive Company.

    The present Chairman of the Board is Mr. J. F. Fairlamb, Auditor of Miscellaneous Accounts, New York Central Railroad.

    While called a Building and Loan Association; its functions extend much beyond the scope of the ordinary building and loan association and it might more properly be described as a Savings Club.

    Any person may invest money in this association in any one or all of the three following ways:

    Regular Shares: Which requires a stated monthly payment of $1.00 a share. These shares are now earning interest at the rate of 6% per year. Dividends are paid semi annually.

    Savings Shares: In which, on the same principle as a savings bank, the member may deposit any sum of money at any time. These share are now earning at the rate of 5% per year. Dividends are paid January 1st and July 1st on all amounts invested for more than three months before these dates.

    Income Shares: Which are issued at any time in amounts of $100 or multiples of that amount. The dividend to be paid is agreed upon at the time of investment and these shares earn from date of deposit.

    Should a member desire to draw on his deposit account he may do so at any time and this does not end his membership or prohibit his further participation.

    All funds received on deposit are invested in first mortgages on improved New York City and suburban real estate within fifty miles of the Grand Central Station. Monthly payments of principal and interest are required on these mortgages, thus constantly increasing the margin of security.

    The Association owns its own building located at 437-439 Lexington Avenue and valued at $238,000, and I am informed that the Association has been successful from the start.

    The Association makes the following statement:
(line missing) “Started in 1890 by a group of railroad and express company employees for the purpose of enabling its members to conserve their savings and acquire homes, it has grown until now it has more than 11,000 depositors. Through its assistance more than 2500 depositors have obtained homes. On our mortgage application for either buying or building a home, our own depositors receive precedence and when mortgage money is in great demand we accept applications only from our own members.”
    I am verbally informed that any member who has deposits aggregating $50.00 or more to his credit may borrow $100.00 from the Association by obtaining in addition to his own signature on the note, the endorsement of any other member who has $50.00 or more on deposit.

    I have other data on this Association which will be furnished if desired.

    In some particulars the plan of the Railroad Cooperative Building and Loan Association seems to me to be more liberal and therefore more attractive to the investor than the plan suggested in Mr. Lane’s letter of November 8th, and since we are invited to criticize the plan which Mr. Lane has suggested, I will take the liberty to point out one of the two features, which possibly might be improved on.

    Article III – Deposits – Sec. 1. Requires a deposit of 5% of the employee’s salary on each pay day by deduction from payroll.

    I would suggest that the entire deduction be made from the pay day which falls on the 16th of the month, as rent, store bills, milk bills, etc., fall due on the first of the month and there is a greater demand on the employe’s purse in connection with the first of the month pay day than the 16th of the month pay day.

    Furthermore, there would be less accounting and less expense if deductions were made only once instead of twice a month.

    Article III – Section 3. I do not like the proposition to impose fines upon employees and wish there could be some way to avoid this as it would not serve to make the plan popular, but on the contrary would prevent some employees from joining and the proposition of imposing fines would be fully exploited by labor agitators.

    Article IV – Loans – Sec. 1. Is there is a real necessity for requiring endorsements on all loans in excess of $25.00, especially in those cases where the borrower has deposited more than the amount of the loan requested.

    Article IV – Section 3. The proposal to charge interest on all loans at the rate of 1% per month, would, I believe arouse much adverse criticism and labor leaders such as Samuel Gompers would immediately claim that The Pullman Company is engaged in the loan shark business at the expense of its employees and Mr. Gompers would probably amplify his criticism to the extent of saying that Pullman employees are so poorly paid that they are forced to borrow money from the Company, which takes advantage of their necessities by extorting loan shark rates of interest. Could we not make the loan rate 7% per annum.

    Article V – Withdrawals – Section 4; reads:
“A depositor who withdraws once cannot reenter and participate in the fund.”
Cannot this section be eliminated or so modified as to take care of contingencies which are certain to arise, and still not debar the employee from further participation.

    There are occasions where the long illness or death of a member of the family, or even the arrival of a new baby may create unusual expenses which the employee could not meet out of his current earnings and which might necessitate his withdrawing the amounts which he had deposited and which had accrued to his credit through his patient and earnest efforts and it would not seem that such unavoidable withdrawal should for ever after debar him from participation or membership in the Savings Club.

    My understanding of the policy of the Management and my own individual belief is that during this period of labor unrest and of extreme activity on the part of various socialistic and communistic organizations, it behooves the Company to strengthen and popularize its own Plan of Employee Representation as the channel through which employees will have representation and a voice in matters affecting their welfare, and if we do not do this there is great danger, proved by our own experience, that our employees will drift into other and antagonistic organizations which are constantly soliciting their membership.

    I would therefore earnestly recommend that these savings clubs be made in some way a part of the Plan of Employee Representation with local administration through our local joint committees, finally a Board of Trustees on which the Bureau of Industrial Relations and Employee Representatives on the General Committees will be represented in the membership.

    In this way a special incentive will be offered to join the Plan of Employee Representation.

    F. L. Simmons

Indigenous Peoples in Post-Revolutionary Mexico, 1920-2005

Seminar Description
This seminar will present Mexican history since the 1910-1920 revolution from the point of view of the more than one million indigenous people who live in Mexico today. Beginning in the 1920s, the increasingly paternalist policies of the Mexican government toward native peoples became known as indigenismo. While these policies ostensibly proposed to uplift Indian communities, many of them aimed instead to acculturate them to dominant Mexican culture. Still, many indigenous communities received their own lands and thus a measure of their own cultural autonomy during the twentieth century. Participants will explore indigenous people's efforts to sustain their communities, cultures, natural environments, and economic autonomy over the course of the twentieth century and into the first years of the twenty-first. They will also discuss the impact of government initiatives such as land reform, indigenismo, and development/conservation of woodlands where most native peoples live, with particular attention to the Tarahumara (Rarámuri) Indians of Chihuahua, the Tarascans (Purépecha) of Michoacán, the Zapotecs of Oaxaca, and the Maya peoples of the Yucatán. Ultimately, participants will consider challenges that confront Mexico's native peoples today: globalization, narcotrafficking, the loss of forestland, and the impact of the Zapatista revolt.

About the Instructor
Christopher Boyer is Associate Professor of History and Latin American and Latino Studies at the University of Illinois at Chicago. He earned his doctorate in 1997 from the University of Chicago. He is the author of Becoming Campesinos: Politics, Identity, and Agrarian Struggle in Michoacan, 1920-1930. His current research draws on both social and environmental history to show how the Mexican government has used scientific understandings of forestry to introduce laws aimed at re-engineering and modernizing the way that rural people use their woodlands, creating a conflict between peasant communities and the central government.

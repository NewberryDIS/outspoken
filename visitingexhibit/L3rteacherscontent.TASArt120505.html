Teaching Evolution in America - The Power of the Scientific Method

Seminar Description
Understanding biological evolution is crucial for all informed citizens, whether they are involved in science, concerned with agricultural and environmental issues, or are interested in politics and the decisions that will shape future governmental policies. Quite remarkably the theory of evolution has been under attack by scientific, cultural and religious institutions since the publication of the initial paper by Darwin and Wallace in 1858. In the intervening years, an overwhelming body of scientific evidence has demonstrated that the modern synthesis of the theory is largely correct. In fact many of the modern advances in agriculture and medicine are direct applications of the principles of the modern synthesis. This seminar will explore three aspects of the dialogue between "Science" and "Culture" as it pertains to the modern synthesis of evolution. First we will explore what modern experiments reveal about the mechanisms of evolution. Second we will consider the nature of scientific questions and differentiate between postulates, hypotheses and theories. Third, we will address the ongoing conversation to explore how scientists and educators can make evolutionary theory more accessible to society as a whole.

About the Instructor
Jonathan Art is Associate Professor in the Department of Anatomy & Cell Biology, and an Associate Dean of the Graduate College at the University of Illinois at Chicago. He earned his doctorate from the Department of Organismal Biology & Anatomy at the University of Chicago for work in comparative physiology and theoretical biology. His postdoctoral research at the Universities of Sussex and Cambridge (U.K.) focused on the biophysical properties of receptor cells in the ear. He currently teaches both medical and graduate students in the College of Medicine, and his research continues to explore the cellular basis of information processing in the auditory and vestibular systems using electrical and optical techniques.

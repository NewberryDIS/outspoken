Bringing The Scarlett Letter to Life

Seminar Description
In his introduction to the book that Henry James would later call "the finest piece of imaginative writing yet put forth in this country," Nathaniel Hawthorne described finding a piece of "fine red cloth, much worn and faded," while "poking and burrowing into the heaped-up rubbish in the corner" of the old Salem Custom House. "It was the capital letter A," Hawthorne explained, and it set him to writing The Scarlet Letter. In this seminar, we will poke and burrow into a few of the Newberry's worn and faded collections to reconstruct the novel's genesis for ourselves. On whom did Hawthorne base his enigmatic heroine, Hester Prynne, and how faithfully did he reconstruct her world? We will not only look back, but forward-finding reasons for the novel's lasting power and ways to bring its formal (not to say faded) prose to life for our students.

About the Instructor
Sarah Fenton is Lecturer and Assistant Chair in History at Northwestern; she has also taught American literature at the Newberry Library and Lake Forest College. She is currently at work on a book entitled Rebel Angels: American Literature across the Civil War. Sarah has appeared on C-SPAN's Great American Writers series and most recently authored several entries for The Encyclopedia of Chicago. This is her third TAS seminar.

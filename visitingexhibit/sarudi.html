National History Project

Southern Rhetoric From Planter Aristocracy During Reconstruction

Researched by Ivan Sarudi, Kenwood Academy

Introduction
     When did the Civil War end for white southerners? How did the former leaders of southern society define Reconstruction? They believed that they had exerted complete control over southern politics, economics, and culture before the war. After, they felt that their entire world had been turned upside down. W. E. B. Dubois has argued that southern planters felt that they were robbed of their identity, especially when lower-class whites and blacks attempted "to imitate the manners of a slave-nurtured gentility."(1) With the emancipation of their slaves, the presence of Northern troops on their soil and their economic future uncertain, many white southern aristocrats resolved to defy and rebel against the new order. The representatives of this segment of Southern society put forth their arguments in pamphlets as a means of an open forum and they were widely circulated. In addition to ex-politicians, judges, doctors, and ministers wrote these tracts. Many of the authors used religious references and enlightenment philosophy to support their arguments for self-government and theories of racial superiority. These pamphlets were powerful tools of persuasion.

Racial Order and Political Power
     For the first part of my project, I wanted to understand what the white southern leadership believed at the onset of the Civil War about these subjects. Written in 1846, Matthew Estes's, A Defence of Negro Slavery, as it exists in the United States provided me with a good example. He defended the institution of slavery by arguing it was a universal economic necessity.(2) He also made a biological justification for the enslavement of blacks. (3) (See Document One) Physician John H. Van Evrie held similar views, which he discussed in his tract, White Supremacy and Negro Subordination; or, Negroes a Subordinate Race, and (so-called) Slavery its Normal Condition. (Although this was published in 1868, he wrote the main text prior to the start of the Civil War.) In his preface, he referred to the content as "a plain, simple, and truthful exposition of the natural order and social adaptation of the white and Negro races, as they existed in the Southern States, and indeed, in all the States, save one, when the Union was formed." Van Evrie argued that freedom would prove detrimental to the well-being of blacks. He claimed that in invading the South, the North had "torn four million . . . negroes from their normal condition, and [were] striving to 'reconstruct' American society on a Mongrel basis, as in Mexico, Central America, etc." (See Images from Van Evrie's Book for Illustration) (For a similar argument regarding the inferiority of Latin America see Kathy Pozniak's paper on "Fugitive Slaves in Mexico.") Van Evrie defiantly contended that "governments cannot exist an hour anywhere where these widely different races are forced into legal equality in approximate proportions."(4) (See Document Two)

Contesting the Reconstruction Amendments
     Southerners resisted the Reconstruction amendments. They still believed in their version of state's rights, however at odds with the federal government it might have been. Consequently, they made arguments that they believed might have a universal appeal; arguments based upon natural law and racism. In a tract entitled, The Practical Philosopher and True Senator, the anonymous author argued that voting was not a constitutional issue. Further, making it an issue by passing the 14th Amendment would put the nation in new peril because he believed that "inferior races who may be an element of our population" posed an even graver danger than former slaves.(5) His list included Chinese, Aztecs, natives of lower California and Mexico, Indians, and the "Mongrel races." This author suggested that if the constitution was to be amended it should be to substitute the words "white" and "duly naturalized" in the place of "free," since, according to him, this word no longer held any political meaning.(6) (See Document Three)

Radical Reconstruction
     Republican congressmen ushered in Radical Reconstruction in response to Southern defiance. Southerners still exploited many freedmen for their labor in an effort to keep things the way they were. In a report to the president on the condition of the South, General Carl Schurz made note of the quick imposition of laws, known as black codes, that restricted the labor opportunities and physical movement of blacks. Schurz believed that the planters had "absolutely no conception of what free labor is."(7) According to historian Charles L. Flynn, Jr., "[slaveholders'] memories of the ideal black slave became indistinguishable from the ideal against which they judged free black workers."(8) Historian Theodore Branter Wilson has argued that the North interpreted the institution of the codes as a "categorical refusal to accept the verdict of the Civil War, a vindictive effort to thwart justice, and a conspiracy to retain the Negro in virtual slavery or peonage."(9) But many white Southerners viewed the codes as vital protection for their women and children and as the only practical way to induce the freedmen to support themselves. Radical Republicans demanded that the South give blacks higher status than that experienced by free blacks in the South prior to the war and, in fact, more liberty than that enjoyed by blacks in the North. Ultimately, as Wilson has contended, "these positions were poles apart and were equally unrealistic."(10)

     Since the South had long ago convinced itself of the inferiority of the "Negro race," it presumed that a separate system of regulations was necessary. The South resented the expectations of the North. An article in the Natchez Democrat on Christmas Day, 1865 captured this vehemence:

"We do not know that obligation requires us to whine, like the whipped spaniel, about the footstool of power, we do not know that our obligations require us to get down upon our knees before the profane altars of an odious fanaticism, and cry 'pecca vimmus! Pecca vimmus! We have sinned! We have sinned!'(11)

     Written in 1871, Concession, or, How the Lost Cause May Be Regained and the Independence of the South Secured, is another excellent example of Southern resistance. The anonymous author presented his readers with a point by point plan for reestablishing southern independence and restoring their old way of life, their beliefs and their institutions. First, he reminded Southerners of the lives already sacrificed to maintain the Southern nation. Second, he argued the except for a common language, the North and South existed as completely separate nations with separate cultures, politics, and economies. Using a racially charged image, he contended that Radical Reconstruction had reduced the "ever proud, chivalric and glorious state of South Carolina . . . into a burlesque African republic." Ultimately, he called for a second rebellion. The mere threat of violence, he said, would be enough to force the Union to grant southerners' their single demand: independence.(12) (See Document Four)

Conclusion
     Abolishing slavery turned Southern aristocracy upside down. Feeling under attack, they interpreted these changes as both humiliating and debilitating. Southern planters found a medium, however, to voice their beliefs and their frustrations. Through pamphlets, Southern planters continued to defy the federal government after the Civil War. Their thoughts and feelings, however wrong and misdirected they may have been, reflect how precious and important their beliefs and way of life was to them.

Exploring Lewis and Clark

Seminar Description
When Thomas Jefferson's Corps of Discovery pushed up the Missouri River in mid-May 1804, William Clark wrote that the expedition's "road across the continent" would take it through "a multitude of Indians." This seminar begins by acknowledging that Clark was right. The West at the beginning of the nineteenth century was Indian Country and the almost-daily encounters between American explorers and native people is at the heart of this first American road story. Seminar members are asked to make a conceptual shift when thinking about Lewis and Clark-to get off the expedition's boats and see events from the Native American bank. This is not an exclusively Indian story but native voices and perspectives are vital for a fuller understanding of this emblematic American journey. The seminar will explore the expedition's origins, its interactions with the American landscape and first peoples, and the complex consequences of the journey.

About the Instructor
James P. Ronda holds the H. G. Barnard Chair in Western American History at the University of Tulsa and is a past president of the Western History Association. A specialist in the history of the exploration of the American West, he is the author of many books, articles, and essays including Lewis and Clark among the Indians, Astoria and Empire, Jefferson's West: A Journey with Lewis and Clark, Finding the West: Explorations with Lewis and Clark, and Beyond Lewis and Clark: The Army Explores the West. In addition to a busy research and writing career, Professor Ronda has served as an on-camera commentator for many radio, television, and film productions about Lewis and Clark. He has also been a principal consultant for a number of museum exhibitions. In 2003 Ronda gave the keynote address at Monticello for the official beginning of the Lewis and Clark Bicentennial.

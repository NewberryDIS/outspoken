Personal Identity and Bioethics

Seminar Description
While advances in medicine and medical technology can be a great boon to humankind, they also frequently raise difficult ethical questions. The range and urgency of these questions is clear in public debates about issues like cloning, stem cell research, and end-of-life concerns. Philosophers working in the area of biomedical ethics strive to find principles that can aid in negotiating the dilemmas we are bound to encounter with increasing frequency as new biotechnologies are developed. Some of the most interesting recent work by bioethicists brings philosophical discussions of personal identity to bear on these questions. Many of the dilemmas these ethicists study take the form of questions about identity-questions about whether the person is still there when someone falls into a vegetative state, or whether someone in the late stages of dementia should be treated as the same person she was before she became ill. In this seminar we will consider how philosophical discussions of personal identity can be applied to questions in biomedical ethics. We will begin with a brief review of what philosophers have had to say about abstract issues of personal identity and then go on to look at particular bioethical dilemmas and the role philosophical accounts of personal identity have played in trying to resolve them.

About the Instructor
Marya Schechtman is an Associate Professor of Philosophy and Associate Dean of the Graduate College at the University of Illinois at Chicago. She is the author of The Constitution of Selves and has published numerous articles in the areas of personal identity and the philosophy of mind. Her research interests include the connection between metaphysical and ethical questions of personal identity, and between philosophy and psychology. She is currently working on a book entitled Staying Alive: Experience, Agency, and Personal Survival.
